Introduction: Least Square Method in Robotics

For pose control and estimation, usually we have a linear form f:Rk→Rf: \mathbb R^k \to \mathbb Rf:Rk→R to evaluate the target value and minimize that value.
The most difficult step is to find a Δx\Delta xΔx, s.t. ∣∣f(x+Δx)−f(x)∣∣2<∣∣f(x)∣∣2||f(x+\Delta x) - f(x) ||^2 < ||f(x)||^2∣∣f(x+Δx)−f(x)∣∣2<∣∣f(x)∣∣2 until the Δx\Delta xΔx is too small.
For example, in the camera coordinate, we have measured pose ppp, and the robot has pose SE TTT, and the measured pose in world coordinate zzz:

z=Tpz = Tp z=Tp

For measurement noise www, we have z=Tp+wz = Tp + wz=Tp+w, and in consideration of measure error, we have e=z−Tpe = z - Tpe=z−Tp, and clearly we need to minimize the error eee or say minimize the displacement error ∣∣e∣∣2||e||_2∣∣e∣∣2​, and what we can modify is the transformation matrix TTT.
By the method of estimation in statistics, the formula would be like the following:

arg⁡min⁡TJ(T)=arg⁡min⁡T∑i=1N∣∣zi−Tpi∣∣22\arg\min_T J(T) = \arg\min_T\sum_{i=1}^N ||z_i - Tp_i||_2^2 argTmin​J(T)=argTmin​i=1∑N​∣∣zi​−Tpi​∣∣22​

Like the method of maximum likelihood estimation, we should calculate the derivative of the function, and clearly the rotation matrix RRR is not closed on the (R,+)(R, +)(R,+) group space, thus it’s our purpose to find a better method, which we will introduced as Lie Theory.

Definition: Lie Algebra

For simple SO(3), the rotation matrix, it holds the invariant:

R(t)RT(t)=IR(t) R^T(t) = I R(t)RT(t)=I

By differentiate both sides, it becomes

R˙(t)RT(t)+R(t)R˙T(t)=0\dot R(t) R^T(t) + R(t)\dot R^T(t) = 0 R˙(t)RT(t)+R(t)R˙T(t)=0

R˙(t)RT(t)=−(R˙(t)RT(t))T\dot R(t)R^T(t) = -(\dot R(t)R^T(t))^T R˙(t)RT(t)=−(R˙(t)RT(t))T

which means that the R˙(t)RT(t)\dot R(t) R^T(t)R˙(t)RT(t) is a anti-symmetric matrix, which thus could be expressed in the form of ϕ(t)∧\phi(t)^{\wedge}ϕ(t)∧. And then we right multiply a R(t)R(t)R(t) to both sides below, and obtain:

R˙(t)RT(t)=ϕ(t)∧⇔R˙(t)=ϕ(t)∧R(t)\dot R(t)R^T(t) = \phi(t)^\wedge \Leftrightarrow \dot R(t) = \phi(t)^\wedge R(t) R˙(t)RT(t)=ϕ(t)∧⇔R˙(t)=ϕ(t)∧R(t)

Namely for any R(t)∈SOR(t)\in SOR(t)∈SO, its derivative is equal to left multiply a anti-symmetric matrix ϕ(t)∧\phi(t)^\wedgeϕ(t)∧
Then we do Taylor Expansion (1 level) near time t0=0t_0=0t0​=0 and get

R(t)=R(t0)+R˙(t0)(t−t0)R(t) = R(t_0) + \dot R(t_0) (t - t_0) R(t)=R(t0​)+R˙(t0​)(t−t0​)

R(t)=I+ϕ(t0)∧(t)R(t) = I + \phi(t_0)^\wedge (t) R(t)=I+ϕ(t0​)∧(t)

and we have that R˙(t)=ϕ(t)∧R(t)\dot R(t) = \phi(t)^\wedge R(t)R˙(t)=ϕ(t)∧R(t), where near t0t_0t0​ the ϕ(t)\phi(t)ϕ(t) is always the same as ϕ(t0)≈ϕ0∧\phi(t_0)\approx \phi_0^\wedgeϕ(t0​)≈ϕ0∧​, with initial value problem R(0)=IR(0) = IR(0)=I.

R(t)=eϕ0∧tR(t) = e^{\phi_0^\wedge t} R(t)=eϕ0∧​t

Since ϕ∧\phi^\wedgeϕ∧ reflect the property of R(t)R(t)R(t), thus it’s in the R(t)R(t)R(t)'s tangent space near ttt
